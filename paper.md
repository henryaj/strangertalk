# Talking to strangers: A week-long intervention reduces psychological barriers to social connection

**Gillian M. Sandstrom, Erica J. Boothby, Gus Cooney**

*Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*, Volume 102, September 2022, 104356

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jesp.2022.104356 — Open access, Creative Commons license

## Abstract

Although people derive substantial benefit from social connection, they often refrain from talking to strangers because they have pessimistic expectations about how such conversations will go (e.g., they believe they will be rejected or not know what to say). Previous research has attempted but failed to get people to realize that their concerns about talking to strangers are overblown. To reduce people's fears, we developed an intervention in which participants played a week-long scavenger hunt game that involved repeatedly finding, approaching, and talking to strangers. Compared to controls, this minimal, easily replicable treatment made people less pessimistic about the possibility of rejection and more optimistic about their conversational ability—and these benefits persisted for at least a week after the study ended. Daily reports revealed that people's expectations grew more positive and accurate by the day, emphasizing the importance of repeated experience in improving people's attitudes towards talking with strangers.

**Keywords:** Social interaction, Conversation, Intervention, Social connection

## Introduction

Research on well-being, conversation, and belonging has underscored the importance of social interaction for people's health and happiness. Despite the benefits of social interaction, people seldom strike up conversations with people they do not know. Instead, people wear headphones to avoid talking, stay glued to their smartphones in public places, or pretend not to notice a new coworker they still have not introduced themselves to. These impressive displays of "civil inattention," seemingly innocuous dodges in the moment, can collectively add up to a behavioral pattern that stymies social interaction before it begins.

Recent research suggests one reason for this behavior: people are remarkably pessimistic about almost every aspect of talking to strangers. For example, people expect that others will not be interested in talking to them, people underestimate how much others like them after meeting for the first time, and people are skeptical of their ability to start and maintain such conversations. In a striking demonstration of this basic idea, Epley and Schroeder (2014) approached people waiting for a train in Chicago and showed that they systematically underestimated how rewarding it would be to strike up a conversation with a stranger during their commute. Our aim in this paper is twofold. First, to replicate past research showing that people underestimate how positively strangers will react to attempts at social connection. And second, to intervene on people's pessimistic beliefs, thereby allowing people to see the possibility of talking to a stranger as a positive opportunity rather than something to dread.

Fortunately, the same research that demonstrates people's pessimism about talking to strangers also shows that this pessimism is often misplaced—strangers are more willing to talk than people anticipate, conversations tend to go overwhelmingly better than people predict, and people are more conversationally competent than they expect. This suggests a promising intervention strategy: give people *concentrated* and *repeated* practice talking to strangers, so that they may realize their fears are exaggerated.

There have been surprisingly few attempts to improve people's attitudes towards talking to strangers, and those attempts have proved only partially successful. Here, we added to this new body of research, and aimed to improve on prior intervention efforts by creating a more lasting shift in people's attitudes. We did so by having people play a scavenger hunt game that prompted them to engage in repeated conversations with strangers over the course of a week. Why a whole week?

It would seem that sporadic positive interactions with strangers, like the ones that people have in their day-to-day lives, do not fully allay people's fears, and that the lessons from such positive interactions do not stick. Perhaps it is too easy for people to discount any single conversation that goes well as a pleasant exception to the unpleasant rule. Or perhaps because people talk to strangers relatively infrequently, it is difficult to detect a consistent pattern. For these reasons, we aimed to alter people's entrenched pessimism by having people repeatedly practice talking over the course of many consecutive days. Overall, our aim was to condition people to the (surprisingly positive) reality of talking to strangers, thereby reducing people's fears, and increasing people's recognition that these conversations typically go quite well.

## 1. Current research

We ran a week-long, multi-site intervention, in which participants were prompted by a scavenger hunt app to either start conversations with, or simply observe, at least one stranger every day for a week. As the intervention unfolded, we measured people's fears about rejection, their beliefs about their conversational ability, their expected awkwardness and enjoyment, and their beliefs about the impression they make on strangers. We aimed to find out whether repeatedly talking to strangers over the course of a week could make people less pessimistic about the prospect of talking to strangers.

## 2. Method

This manuscript reports the results of a time-consuming and resource-intensive intervention. Two consequences are that: (a) we pre-registered several research questions that could be answered with the resulting data; and (b) there were some slight deviations from our pre-registered plan.

- **(a)** This paper reports the results of one of our pre-registered research questions (Q1): "Do repeated interactions with strangers improve attitudes towards talking to strangers?" (see https://osf.io/dvqez/). Correspondingly, we analyze only the measures that are associated with this research question.

- **(b)** *Analyses.* Although we did not conduct the exact analyses that we had planned, they were conceptually very similar: we used condition, time, and condition x time as predictors of our outcomes, and focused on the specified comparisons that we pre-registered (baseline vs. end-of-study, and baseline vs. follow-up). For analyses of rejection, our count data were over-dispersed, so we used a negative binomial regression to analyze these data. Upon further reflection we also chose a regression framework to analyze the other outcomes as well, instead of using the ANOVA framework that we had pre-registered.

Sample size was determined before any data analysis, and we report all manipulations and exclusions.

### 2.1. Participants

A total of 454 people started the study. Twenty-one were removed due to experimenter error. Of the remaining 433, 136 were removed due to pre-registered exclusion criteria: 68 completed fewer than four days (12% control; 17% treatment), and a further 68 failed the honesty check (11% control, 18% treatment). We analyze the responses of the remaining **286 people** (75 male, 209 female, 2 identified otherwise or preferred not to say; M_age = 20.1 years, SD = 2.1 years).

This sample was recruited from two university campuses—one in the U.S. (N = 135) and one in the U.K. (N = 151). A sensitivity analysis suggests the sample can detect effects where f >= 0.08 (i.e., small effects).

### 2.2. Procedure

We created a scavenger hunt game using a mobile app called GooseChase. Participants used the app to complete the study. We designed **29 scavenger hunt "missions"** that people could choose from. The goal of each mission was to find a stranger with certain characteristics (e.g., "find someone wearing interesting shoes" or "find someone who's drinking a coffee"; see Appendix for full list). Participants received points on the app and an entry into a draw prize for every mission completed. The app allowed participants to see their performance compared to other participants.

Participants were recruited in weekly groups. On Monday, participants completed surveys and received instructions. After the lab visit, all communication occurred by text message and email. All members of that week's group were assigned to the same condition:

- **Treatment condition** (N = 198): find, approach, and *talk to* a stranger
- **Control condition** (N = 88): find, approach, but simply *observe* a stranger

Participants in the treatment condition either saw tips in their mission descriptions (N = 98) or not (N = 100); as pre-registered, these were collapsed into a single treatment condition. In total, participants in the treatment condition had **1336 conversations** with strangers.

### 2.3. Measures

For one week—daily from Monday to Friday—participants played the scavenger hunt game. Participants completed both "General" and "Daily" surveys.

**General surveys** captured broader attitudinal and behavioral changes, completed at:
- *Start of study* (Monday)
- *End of study* (Friday)
- *Follow-up* (one week after intervention ended)

**Daily surveys** were completed at the beginning of each day (*pre-conversation*) and after each mission (*post-conversation*). Our primary interest was examining the time course of the intervention and the underlying psychological processes.

#### 2.3.1. Rejection

We asked participants whether they thought the people they approached would be willing to talk to them. "How many people do you think you will need to approach in order to complete a mission (i.e., get someone to talk to you)?" Participants entered "1" if they thought the first person would talk to them. We created a measure of *rejection* by subtracting one from the number of expected/actual approaches.

#### 2.3.2. Conversational ability

We asked participants how skilled they were in talking to strangers ("It is hard to start a conversation / to keep a conversation going / to end a conversation with a stranger"). We created composite measures of *conversational ability* assessed on a 7-point scale, reverse-coded to yield a measure of conversational "ability."

#### 2.3.3. Awkwardness and enjoyment

We assessed both negative and positive feelings about talking to strangers:

- **Awkwardness**: "I feel comfortable (reverse-scored) / awkward / nervous talking to strangers"
- **Enjoyment**: "I enjoy talking to strangers," "When I talk to strangers, I find them interesting / like them," "Talking to strangers feels like work (reverse-scored) / fun"

General measures were assessed on a 7-point scale; Daily measures on a 5-point scale.

#### 2.3.4. Positive impression

Participants reported what kind of impression they would make ("The strangers I talk to like me / find me interesting / enjoy talking to me"). General measure on 7-point scale, Daily on 5-point scale.

#### 2.3.5. Initiating conversations with strangers

Exploratory measure: "How many strangers have YOU started a conversation with in the past 7 days?" Included at start of study and follow-up only.

#### 2.3.6. Noticing opportunities to talk to strangers

Exploratory measure on a 7-point scale: "I notice opportunities to talk to strangers."

## 3. Results

### 3.1. General results: Change in perceived likelihood of rejection

At the end of the week-long intervention, participants in the treatment group expected to be rejected by significantly fewer people than they had at the start of the study (p < .001). Additionally, one week after the intervention had ended, participants in the treatment group *still* expected to be rejected by fewer people, showing a lasting effect (p < .001).

Participants in the control condition predicted that they would be rejected by as many people as participants in the treatment group had predicted at the start of the study. At the end of the study *and* a full week later, participants in the control condition resembled the treatment condition at baseline—overly pessimistic expectations about rejection.

Directly comparing conditions: treatment group expected significantly less rejection at both end of study (p < .001) and follow-up (p < .001).

### 3.2. General results: Change in conversational ability

Participants in the treatment group felt more positive about their ability to talk to strangers at end of study (p < .001) and follow-up (p < .001). Control group did not experience the same improvement. At baseline, groups did not differ (p = .88), but at end of study and follow-up, the treatment group felt more positive (p = .01, p = .004).

### 3.3. General results: Change in awkwardness

Treatment group felt less awkward at end of study (p < .001) and follow-up (p < .001). Control group reported no change. At baseline no difference (p = .87), but treatment felt less awkward at end of study (p = .05) and follow-up (p = .03).

### 3.4. General results: Change in enjoyment

Treatment group expected more enjoyment at end of study (p < .001) and follow-up (p = .002). Control group showed no change. However, directly comparing conditions revealed no significant difference at any time point.

### 3.5. General results: Change in making a positive impression

Treatment group expected a more positive impression at end of study (p < .001) and follow-up (p < .001). Control condition also showed smaller but significant effects (end: p = .01, follow-up: p = .004). However, direct comparison between conditions was not significant.

It appears that simply approaching and observing strangers also made people less pessimistic about the impressions they would make.

### 3.6. Exploratory analyses

#### 3.6.1. Initiating conversations

At follow-up, treatment group reported more conversations with strangers than before the study (p = .01), while control group reported no change (p = .88). However, direct comparison between conditions was not significant.

#### 3.6.2. Noticing opportunities

Treatment group noticed more opportunities at end of study and follow-up (p < .001). Control group did not (ps > .99). Direct comparison between conditions was not significant (ps > .22).

### 3.7. Daily results: Predictions vs. experiences

After undergoing the intervention, participants displayed less pessimistic attitudes. The daily data answer two critical questions:

1. **What is the time course of the intervention on people's beliefs?**
2. **How do people's beliefs compare to reality?**

### 3.8. Daily results: Change in perceived likelihood of rejection

Participants expected significantly more rejection than they experienced (p < .001). On the first day, only **40%** thought the first person would talk to them, but participants managed to have a conversation with the first person they approached **92%** of the time. Over the study, of 1336 conversations, **1164 (87%)** occurred with the first person approached.

There was a significant rating type x time interaction (p < .001). Predicted rejections decreased over time (p < .001), while actual rejections did not change (p = .37). In other words, the number of rejections people actually experienced was consistently low, and predictions decreased to more accurately reflect reality.

### 3.9. Daily results: Change in conversational ability

Participants expected lower conversational ability than they actually reported. There was a significant rating type x time interaction (p < .001). Both predicted and actual ability increased, but predicted ability increased faster, closing the pessimism gap.

### 3.10. Daily results: Change in awkwardness

Participants expected to feel more awkward than they actually did. Significant rating type x time interaction (p = .003). Both predicted and actual awkwardness decreased, with predicted decreasing faster.

### 3.11. Daily results: Change in enjoyment

Participants expected less enjoyment than they experienced. However, the rating type x time interaction was not significant (p = .37)—the intervention did not have a discernible effect on the prediction-reality gap for enjoyment.

### 3.12. Daily results: Change in making a positive impression

Participants expected to make a less positive impression than they actually did. The rating type x time interaction was not significant (p = .15). Both predicted and actual positive impression increased over time.

Overall, the daily survey results are broadly consistent with the proposal that the intervention worked by *repeatedly* conditioning people to the reality of talking to strangers.

## 4. Discussion

People are remarkably pessimistic about talking to strangers. Our "talking to strangers" intervention aimed to reduce these fears by prompting repeated conversations over five days. By the end, the treatment group reported significantly more positive attitudes: less anticipated rejection, less awkwardness, and more confidence in their conversational ability—effects that persisted at least one week after the intervention ended.

The intervention also improved enjoyment expectations and impression expectations, though the control condition also showed modest improvements on these two measures.

Daily surveys highlighted the dimensions where the intervention was most effective: **fear of rejection, conversational ability, and awkwardness**. People's overly pessimistic expectations grew more positive and more accurate by the day.

### 4.1. What features contributed to effectiveness?

#### 4.1.1. Repeated practice

The intervention prompted *repeated* conversations over a week. People's beliefs changed gradually—past research suggests that after a single pleasant conversation, people fail to generalize, returning to pessimistic expectations. Repeated practice appears to be a critical ingredient.

This emphasis on repeated experience is reminiscent of cognitive behavioral therapies, though without explicit cognitive restructuring. Intriguingly, there is evidence that explicit cognitive restructuring is less important than practice for treating anxiety and social phobias.

#### 4.1.2. Gamification

The scavenger hunt game format likely contributed to effectiveness. Features included:
- Choice of which missions to tackle each day
- Points for each completed mission
- Leaderboard showing relative rankings

These gamification features helped participants see talking to strangers as a challenge rather than a threat.

### 4.2. Limitations

- We don't know how well effects generalize beyond the gamified delivery format
- Some attrition occurred; participants who dropped out did not differ from finishers on personality traits, but some reported more negative experiences on day one
- The intervention reduced negative outcomes (fear of rejection) more strongly than it improved positive outcomes (impression expectations)—an unexpected and unexplained asymmetry

### 4.3. Implications for encouraging more conversations

The intervention shifted attitudes, norm perceptions, and perceived behavioral control—all important predictors in models of behavior change. Preliminary support: treatment participants reported talking to more strangers in the follow-up week. However, this measure was imprecise and exploratory.

### 4.4. Implications for loneliness

The intervention is relevant to the growing loneliness crisis. It operates across multiple intervention categories:

1. **Increases opportunities** for social interaction by making people more aware of existing opportunities
2. **Disrupts maladaptive cognition**, allowing people to develop less pessimistic narratives
3. **Improves perceived social skills**, even without explicit instruction

Notably, **41% of treatment participants** reported exchanging contact information and following up with at least one conversation partner.

### 4.5. Conclusion

Our "talking to strangers" intervention provides evidence that repeated experience talking to strangers can reduce people's fears and make their predictions about future conversations more accurate.

At its heart, this intervention is simple: it involves repeatedly approaching and talking to strangers. As such, it is something many people could self-administer. As our research shows, these conversations really do get easier with practice, and the experience will be more positive than you expect.
